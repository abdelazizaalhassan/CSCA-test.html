Classical Probability — The Foundation

What Is Classical Probability?

Classical probability applies when all outcomes in the sample space are equally likely. It is the simplest and most commonly tested model on the CSCA.

P(A) = Number of favorable outcomes / Total number of equally likely outcomes

0 ≤ P(A) ≤ 1   always  |  P(impossible) = 0  |  P(certain) = 1
The Three-Step Process for Any Probability Question Step 1 — Define the sample space: List or count ALL possible outcomes
Step 2 — Identify favorable outcomes: Which outcomes satisfy the event?
Step 3 — Compute the ratio: P(A) = favorable / total

Building Sample Spaces — The Key Skill

On the CSCA, questions always give you a physical setup (balls in a bag, people chosen, apps selected). Your first job is always to correctly identify the total number of outcomes.

Lesson 4.2

Counting Techniques — Combinations

Why Counting Is the Core Skill

Every classical probability question reduces to two counts: how many total ways, and how many favorable ways. Getting these wrong means the probability is wrong.

Combination (order doesn't matter):
C(n, r) = n! / (r! × (n−r)!)

Read as "n choose r" — selecting r items from n items

C(5,2) = 5!/(2!×3!) = (5×4)/(2×1) = 10
C(4,2) = 4!/(2!×2!) = (4×3)/(2×1) = 6
C(n,1) = n   C(n,0) = 1   C(n,n) = 1
When to Use Combinations Use C(n,r) when: order does NOT matter and items are chosen without replacement.
Drawing 2 balls from a bag → C(5,2) = 10 (the pair {R1,R2} is the same regardless of which you picked first).
Selecting a committee of 3 from 10 people → C(10,3) = 120.

Multiplication Principle — Counting Multi-Stage Events

When an event has multiple independent stages, multiply the counts at each stage.

Multiplication Principle If Stage 1 has m ways and Stage 2 has n ways (independent), total = m × n.

Jan 2026 Q48: Choose 1 from high school students AND 1 from college students.
These are two independent choices → multiply probabilities at each stage.

Lesson 4.3

Complement Rule & Compound Events

The Complement Rule — Your Most Powerful Shortcut

Often it is much easier to count what you do NOT want, then subtract from 1.

P(A) = 1 − P(Ā)

Ā = "not A" = complement of A
Use when: "at least one", "at least two", "not all" appear in the problem
When to Immediately Reach for the Complement Trigger words: "at least one", "at least two", "not all same"

P(at least one success) = 1 − P(zero successes)
P(at least 2 of 3 share zodiac) = 1 − P(all 3 have different zodiac signs)

The complement is almost always far easier to count than the event itself.

Addition Rule — Mutually Exclusive vs Overlapping Events

Mutually Exclusive (cannot both happen)
P(A or B) = P(A) + P(B)

Example: rolling a 3 OR a 5 on one die.
P = 1/6 + 1/6 = 2/6 = 1/3
Overlapping (can both happen)
P(A or B) = P(A) + P(B) − P(A and B)

Example: drawing a red OR a King from a deck.
P = 26/52 + 4/52 − 2/52 = 28/52

The complement (all different) has only ONE configuration to count: sequential restriction. Direct counting would require listing all possible sign combinations.

P(yellow or green) = P(Y) + P(G) only if yellow and green are different colors and cannot happen simultaneously in a single draw — which they cannot. This one is fine. But always check.

Lesson 4.4

Conditional Probability & Independent Events

Independence — The Key Concept for Multi-Group Problems

Two events A and B are independent if knowing A happened tells you nothing about B. This is the most important concept for the Jan 2026 Q48 style questions.

If A and B are independent: P(A and B) = P(A) × P(B)

This is the Multiplication Rule for independent events.

P(A | B) = P(A)    (knowing B happened doesn't change P(A))
When Independence Applies on the CSCA The exam explicitly states "preferences are independent of each other" in Jan 2026 Q48.
This means: the high school student's choice and the college student's choice are completely unrelated.
So: P(HS chooses A AND college chooses A) = P(HS chooses A) × P(college chooses A)

With independence, you always multiply the individual probabilities.

Conditional Probability — P(A | B)

P(A | B) = P(A and B) / P(B)

Read as: "probability of A given that B has already occurred"
Geometric Interpretation of Conditional Probability Once we know B occurred, the sample space shrinks to just the outcomes in B.
P(A|B) asks: "Among all outcomes in B, what fraction also belong to A?"

Example: Given a card is red, P(it is a King) = 2/26 = 1/13.
(2 red Kings out of 26 red cards)

Lesson 4.5

Mean, Variance & Numerical Characteristics of Data

Mean (Average) — Measuring the Center

The mean is the arithmetic average — the "balance point" of the data. It tells you where the data clusters.

Population mean: μ = (x₁ + x₂ + … + xₙ) / n = Σxᵢ / n

For grouped/frequency data:
μ = Σ(xᵢ × fᵢ) / Σfᵢ    where fᵢ = frequency of value xᵢ

Variance — Measuring the Spread

Variance measures how far the data points are from the mean on average. A large variance means data is spread out; small variance means data clusters near the mean.

Variance: σ² = Σ(xᵢ − μ)² / n

Equivalent formula (easier to compute):
σ² = (Σxᵢ²)/n − μ²

Standard Deviation: σ = √(σ²)
Step-by-Step Variance Calculation Step 1: Find the mean μ
Step 2: For each data point, compute (xᵢ − μ)²
Step 3: Average these squared deviations: divide by n
Step 4: That is σ². Take √ for standard deviation σ.
Worked Example — Computing Mean and Variance
1
Data: 2, 4, 4, 6, 8, 8, 10 (n=7)
2
Mean: μ = (2+4+4+6+8+8+10)/7 = 42/7 = 6
3
Deviations: (2−6)²=16, (4−6)²=4, (4−6)²=4, (6−6)²=0, (8−6)²=4, (8−6)²=4, (10−6)²=16
4
Sum of squared deviations = 16+4+4+0+4+4+16 = 48
5
Variance: σ² = 48/7 ≈ 6.86.   Standard deviation: σ = √(48/7)

Properties of Mean and Variance — What Changes, What Doesn't

Operation on all dataEffect on MeanEffect on Variance
Add constant c to every valueμ increases by cNo change (σ² unchanged)
Subtract constant c from every valueμ decreases by cNo change (σ² unchanged)
Multiply every value by constant kμ multiplied by kσ² multiplied by k²
Divide every value by constant kμ divided by kσ² divided by k²
Key Insight — Why Adding a Constant Doesn't Change Variance Variance measures spread — how far points are from the mean. If you add 5 to every value, the mean also increases by 5. Each point is still the same distance from its new mean as it was from the old mean. The spread is unchanged. Variance is about relative distances, not absolute positions.

Median and Mode — Other Measures of Center

Median
The middle value when data is sorted.
For odd n: the (n+1)/2 th value.
For even n: average of the two middle values.

Median is robust — not affected by extreme outliers.
Mode
The most frequently occurring value.
A data set can have no mode, one mode, or multiple modes.

Mode is useful for categorical data (e.g., most popular app).
⚠ Common Mistakes — Mean & Variance
✗ Confusing variance σ² with standard deviation σ
✓ Variance = (standard deviation)². Standard deviation = √variance.
If the exam asks for variance, give σ². If it asks for standard deviation, give σ = √(σ²). Don't mix them up.
✗ Adding a constant changes the variance
✓ Adding a constant to all values shifts the mean but leaves variance completely unchanged
Variance measures spread, not location. Shifting all data by the same amount doesn't change how spread out it is.

Normal Distribution — Concepts & Properties

What Is the Normal Distribution?

The normal distribution (bell curve) is the most important probability distribution in statistics. It describes many natural phenomena: heights, exam scores, measurement errors, and more.

The Bell Curve — Key Visual Features • Symmetric about the mean μ — left and right halves are mirror images
• Bell-shaped — highest probability density at the center (mean), tapering toward both tails
• Mean = Median = Mode — all three measures of center coincide
• Total area under curve = 1 (represents 100% probability)
• The curve extends infinitely in both directions but never touches the x-axis

The 68–95–99.7 Rule (Empirical Rule)

For any normal distribution with mean μ and standard deviation σ:

Within 1σ of mean:   P(μ−σ < X < μ+σ) ≈ 68%
Within 2σ of mean:   P(μ−2σ < X < μ+2σ) ≈ 95%
Within 3σ of mean:   P(μ−3σ < X < μ+3σ) ≈ 99.7%

Each tail beyond 3σ contains only 0.15% of data
Applying the 68-95-99.7 Rule
1
Heights of adult men: μ=175cm, σ=8cm. What % are between 159 and 191cm?
2
159 = 175 − 2×8 = μ − 2σ.   191 = 175 + 2×8 = μ + 2σ.
3
This is the range within 2σ → approximately 95% of men have heights in this range.

Standard Normal Distribution — Z-Scores

Any normal distribution can be converted to the standard normal (μ=0, σ=1) using a Z-score transformation.

Z = (X − μ) / σ

Z tells you how many standard deviations X is from the mean.
Z = 0 → exactly at the mean
Z = +1 → one standard deviation above the mean
Z = −2 → two standard deviations below the mean
Properties of the Standard Normal • Mean μ = 0, Standard deviation σ = 1
• P(Z < 0) = P(Z > 0) = 0.5 (symmetric about 0)
• P(−1 < Z < 1) ≈ 0.68
• P(Z < a) = 1 − P(Z < −a) (by symmetry)
• All probabilities looked up via Z-table (standard normal table)

Normal Distribution and Real-World Applications

Quality Control
A factory produces bolts with μ=10mm, σ=0.1mm. Bolts between 9.8 and 10.2mm are acceptable.

9.8 = μ−2σ, 10.2 = μ+2σ → 95% acceptable.
Test Scores
Exam scores: μ=70, σ=10. What score is in the top 16%?

Top 16% is above μ+σ (since 84% is below μ+σ by 68% rule). Score = 70+10 = 80.
CSCA Exam Focus for Normal Distribution The CSCA mostly tests conceptual understanding of the normal distribution, not detailed calculations from Z-tables. Focus on:
• Knowing the shape is symmetric bell curve
• Mean = median = mode for normal distribution
• The 68-95-99.7 rule percentages
• Understanding that larger σ = wider/flatter bell; smaller σ = taller/narrower bell
⚠ Common Mistakes — Normal Distribution
✗ Thinking P(X = exactly 5) is positive for a normal distribution
✓ For any continuous distribution, P(X = exact value) = 0. Only P(a < X < b) is positive.
The normal distribution is continuous — there are infinitely many values, so any single point has probability zero. You can only compute probabilities for intervals.
✗ Thinking the normal curve touches zero at ±3σ
✓ The curve approaches but never actually reaches the x-axis — it extends to ±∞
The tails of the normal distribution are asymptotic to the x-axis. In theory, any value is possible (with tiny but positive probability).
